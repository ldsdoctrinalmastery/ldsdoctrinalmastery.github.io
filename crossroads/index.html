Open · Before We Begin

Three small things.

Two warm-ups and a question — to settle the room and tee up the lesson.

1

A Moment of Gratitude

Bring three things to mind, one at a time:

  • A fun trip you've been on.
  • A delicious meal you've had.
  • A wonderful gift from a parent, leader, or mentor.

Pick one. Now picture everything that went into making it happen — the funds someone provided, the opportunity opened, the research, the planning, the work, the love. Sit with that for a moment.

Now: pull out your phone and text the person who made it happen. Thank them. Right now, while we wait. Gratitude attitude.
2

Loving-Kindness

Close your eyes for thirty seconds. Silently offer the same simple wish — to four different people in turn:

  1. To yourself
  2. To someone you love
  3. To someone you barely know
  4. To someone who has been hard to love

"May they be safe. May they be loved. May they be at peace. May they be truly happy."

Stuck? Picture someone whose love for you is uncomplicated — a sleeping puppy, a small child, a mentor smiling — and let that warmth be the place you wish from.

3

A Question to Open the Floor

When were parents first concerned about technology?

It goes back a lot further than smartphones. Or TV. Or radio.

A few historical worries (for the teacher)
  • ~370 BC — Plato (in Phaedrus) had Socrates warn that writing would destroy memory and produce people who only seemed wise.
  • 1700s — Newspapers and novels were blamed for moral decline, especially among young women.
  • 1950s — Comic books were said to cause juvenile delinquency (Senate hearings, public burnings).
  • 1960s–80s — Television, then rock music, then video arcades.
  • 1990s — The Internet, AOL, chat rooms.
  • 2000s–10s — Halo, World of Warcraft, Facebook, smartphones, TikTok.
  • 2020s — Screentime studies, AI companions, neural implants.

Every generation has its tech panic. Some panics turned out warranted; some did not. Discuss: how do we tell the difference?

A truth
The Lord provides help to guide us in our lives.
And one more thing

This isn't just about phones.

Today's lesson uses "technology" loosely. Phones, social media, scrolling are the obvious examples — but the same principles apply to VR goggles, smart glasses, large language models, AI companions, neural brain implants, robot friends, virtual buddies, and whatever's coming next that we haven't even named yet. The question stays the same regardless of the medium: does it bring you closer to God and to other people, or further away?

II · Three Principles

Purpose. Plan. Pause.

From the Church's Taking Charge of Technology guide. Today we'll focus on the first — but each card below has questions and practical suggestions for all three.

Read the full guide
Today

Purpose

"I can use technology with a purpose. It doesn't control me."

Questions to ask

  • Why am I using technology right now?
  • Do I feel good about what I'm doing?
  • How long will I be using technology?
  • What is my plan for using technology?
  • What am I showing God by how I use my time?

Try it

  • Send a positive message to someone.
  • Listen to music that brings peace.
  • Create your own content.
  • Use it to learn something on purpose.

Plan

"When I plan ahead, I feel better and make better choices."

Questions to ask

  • Am I avoiding content I know isn't right or purposeful?
  • Do my filters and limits match what I actually want?
  • Am I spending time with people, or with feeds about people?

Try it

  • Set a daily limit for screen time.
  • "Follow" only close family and friends.
  • Make a device-free area at home.
  • Set up a family charging station out of bedrooms.
  • Use a content filter.

Pause

"It's OK for me to pause and take a break."

Questions to ask

  • Am I feeling the Spirit leave?
  • Is what I'm seeing making me feel bad, lonely, or weird?
  • Have I stopped enjoying this and just kept going?

Try it

  • Put the device down and step away.
  • Pray for strength.
  • Talk to someone — in person if you can.

VIII · Going Deeper

Things as They Really Are.

Elder David A. Bednar gave two warnings about technology — fifteen years apart. That was then. This is now.

2009 · That Was Then

Things as They Really Are

CES Fireside · BYU-Idaho · May 3, 2009

I raise an apostolic voice of warning about the potentially stifling, suffocating, suppressing, and constraining impact of some kinds of cyberspace interactions and experiences upon our souls.
Read Watch
2024 · This Is Now

Things as They Really Are 2.0

Worldwide Devotional for Young Adults · Nov 3, 2024

An AI companion is only a mathematical algorithm. It does not like you. It does not care about you. It does not really know if you exist or not. … It is a set of computer equations that will treat you as an object to be acted upon, if you let it.
Read

AI is cloaked in the credibility and promises of scientific progress… we might naively be seduced into surrendering our precious moral agency to a technology that can only think telestial. By so doing, we may gradually be transformed from agents who can act into objects that are only acted upon.

Artificial intelligence cannot simulate, imitate, or replace the influence of the Holy Ghost in our lives. No matter how sophisticated and elegant AI technology ultimately may become, it simply can never bear witness of the Father and the Son, reveal the truth of all things, or sanctify those who have repented and been baptized.

— Elder Bednar, Things as They Really Are 2.0

One of my great concerns is that overreliance on AI technology will cause us to become spiritually slothful and shallow — and to forfeit the blessings made possible through righteous work.

A simple command spoken into your digital device, wait a few seconds or minutes, and you have what you need. But do you really have what you need?

— Elder Bednar, Things as They Really Are 2.0

Bednar's own practice

"I personally do not use this technology to generate or draft my talks, articles, or content for other projects. This must be my diligent effort, my creative work, and, most importantly, my seeking to be open to inspiration from the Holy Ghost."

"I do use this technology to critique my work."

He used AI to analyze the tone of this very talk after writing it — and made several changes based on the feedback. The model: AI as a coach or editor, never as a ghostwriter.

  2. Does the time you spend using various technologies and media enlarge or restrict your capacity to live, to love, and to serve in meaningful ways?
